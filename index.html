    Module 1: Phishing and Online Scams

    Objective: To educate students on phishing, online scams, and how to recognize fraudulent communications.
    
    Content:
    What is phishing?
    Phishing is a serious and pervasive cyber threat that involves the fraudulent solicitation of sensitive information from unsuspecting individuals.
    Perpetrators employ deceptive tactics, including spoofed emails, text messages, and social engineering, to masquerade as legitimate entities, such 
    as financial institutions or online service providers. These malicious communications often exploit psychological vulnerabilities, creating a false
    sense of urgency or alarm, which prompts victims to divulge confidential data or click on compromised links. To mitigate the risks associated with
    phishing, it is imperative to implement robust security measures, including multi-factor authentication, regular software updates, and employee 
    education programs. Furthermore, individuals must remain vigilant, scrutinizing emails and messages for suspicious content, and reporting potential
    threats to the relevant authorities.
    
    Types of phishing attacks (email, SMS, fake websites).
   
    Email Phishing
    1. Deceptive Phishing: Fake emails that appear to come from legitimate sources.
    2. Spear Phishing: Targeted attacks on specific individuals or organizations.
    3. Whaling: Targeted attacks on high-level executives or decision-makers.
    4. Mass Phishing: Large-scale email campaigns sent to random recipients.
    
    SMS Phishing (Smishing)
    1. Text Message Phishing: Scammers send fake texts asking for sensitive info.
    2. SMS Spoofing: Scammers disguise their number as a trusted source.
   
    Fake Websites
    1. Phishing Websites: Fake sites that mimic legitimate ones to steal login credentials.
    2. Drive-by Downloads: Malware-infected sites that download viruses onto devices.
    3. URL Spoofing: Scammers disguise malicious URLs as legitimate ones.
    Other Types of Phishing
    1. Voice Phishing (Vishing): Scammers call victims, posing as trusted sources.
    2. Social Media Phishing: Scammers use social media platforms to phish.
    3. QR Code Phishing: Scammers use QR codes to direct victims to malicious sites.
    4. Angela Phishing: Scammers use compromised email accounts to phish.
    5. Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Phishing: Scammers intercept communication between two parties.
    Common online scams (scholarships, job offers, e-commerce fraud).
    
    Scholarship Scams:
    1. Fake scholarship programs asking for payment or personal info.
    2. "Guaranteed" scholarship offers requiring upfront fees.
    3. Phony scholarship websites or emails mimicking legitimate organizations.
    
    Job Offer Scams:
    1. Fake job postings asking for payment or personal info.
    2. "Work-from-home" scams requiring upfront fees or equipment purchases.
    3. Phony job interviews or recruitment agencies demanding payment.
    
    E-commerce Fraud:
    1. Fake online stores or product listings.
    2. Scammers posing as sellers, asking for payment outside of platform.
    3. Phony product reviews or ratings.
    
    Other Common Scams:
    1. Phishing emails or texts impersonating banks, gov't agencies, or companies.
    2. Romance scams: fake profiles, asking for money or personal info.
    3. Investment scams: fake investment opportunities promising high returns.
    4. Lottery and prize scams: fake winnings requiring payment or personal info.
    5. Tech support scams: fake support teams asking for remote access or payment. 

    How to identify and avoid phishing attempts.
    1. Urgency: Be cautious of messages that create a sense of urgency.
    2. Spelling and Grammar: Legitimate organizations usually have professional communications.
    3. Suspicious Sender: Verify the sender's email address.
    4. Fake Links: Hover over links to check the URL.
    5. Attachments: Avoid downloading attachments from unknown senders.
    6. Request for Sensitive Info: Legitimate organizations won't ask for sensitive info via email.

    Real-life examples and preventive measures.
    Real-Life Examples:

    1. Email Phishing:
    - Example: A fake email from "Amazon" claiming your account has been compromised and asking you to reset your password.
    - Prevention: Verify sender authenticity, check for spelling errors, and avoid clicking on suspicious links.

    2. SMS Phishing (Smishing):
        - Example: A text message claiming to be from your bank, asking you to update your account information.
        - Prevention: Be cautious with texts from unknown numbers, don't click on links, and contact your bank directly.

    3. Whaling:
        - Example: A fake email to a CEO, claiming to be from a lawyer, requesting sensitive company information.
        - Prevention: Verify sender authenticity, use two-factor authentication, and educate employees on phishing tactics.

    Preventive Measures:

        Individual Solutions:
        1. Use strong, unique passwords.
        2. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA).
        3. Monitor accounts and credit reports.
        4. Use reputable antivirus software.
        5. Avoid using public Wi-Fi.

        Technological Solutions:
        1. Anti-phishing software (e.g., Norton Anti-Phishing).
        2. Browser extensions (e.g., uBlock Origin).
        3. Email filters (e.g., SpamAssassin).
        4. Two-factor authentication tools (e.g., Authy).
        5. Password managers (e.g., LastPass)
    

    Module 2: Cyberbullying and Harassment

    Objective: To raise awareness about cyberbullying, its effects, and how to deal with it.
Content:
Definition and types of cyberbullying (harassment, shaming, threats).
Cyberbullying constitutes a significant and burgeoning concern within the digital sphere, impacting a substantial number of individuals globally. This malicious behavior entails the utilization of technology to harass, intimidate, or humiliate others through various online platforms, including social media, messaging applications, and online gaming communities. The repercussions of cyberbullying can be severe, culminating in emotional distress, anxiety, depression, and, in extreme cases, suicidal ideation. Furthermore, the anonymity afforded by the internet often emboldens perpetrators, rendering it challenging for victims to escape the relentless stream of harmful messages, images, or comments. To effectively counter this growing issue, it is imperative to promote digital literacy, empathy, and responsible online conduct, while concurrently providing support and resources for victims and holding perpetrators accountable for their actions.

Module 3: Identity Theft and Account Security

Objective: To teach students about the risks of identity theft and securing personal accounts.
Content:
What is identity theft?
Identity theft is a serious crime where an individual's personal and sensitive information, such as name, social security number, credit card details, or passport information, is stolen and used without their consent. This stolen information can be used to commit financial fraud, open new accounts, obtain loans or credit cards, or even gain employment. Identity thieves may obtain personal information through various means, including phishing scams, data breaches, stolen wallets or documents, or hacking into secure databases. Victims of identity theft often suffer financial loss, damage to their credit score, and emotional distress. To protect themselves, individuals must be vigilant in safeguarding their personal information, monitoring their financial accounts, and reporting any suspicious activity to the authorities.
How cybercriminals steal personal data (password theft, social engineering).
Password Theft:
1. Phishing: Fake emails, texts, or messages that trick victims into revealing passwords.
2. Keyloggers: Malware that records keystrokes.
3. Brute Force Attacks: Automated password guessing.
4. Password Cracking: Using software to crack weak passwords.
Social Engineering:
1. Impersonation: Posing as trusted individuals or organizations.
2. Pretexting: Creating fake scenarios to gain trust.
3. Baiting: Leaving malware-infected devices or storage media.
4. Quid Pro Quo: Offering services in exchange for sensitive info.

Other Tactics:
1. Data Breaches: Hacking into databases or systems.
2. Malware: Viruses, Trojans, spyware, and ransomware.
3. Drive-by Downloads: Malware-infected websites.
4. SQL Injection: Exploiting database vulnerabilities.
5. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS): Injecting malicious code.
The importance of strong passwords and two-factor authentication.
Strong Passwords:
Importance:
1. Prevents unauthorized access
2. Protects sensitive information
3. Deters cyber attacks
Characteristics:
1. Length: 12+ characters
2. Complexity: Mix uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and special characters
3. Uniqueness: Use different passwords for each account
4. Avoid: Easily guessable info (name, birthdate, common words)
Two-Factor Authentication (2FA):
Importance:
1. Adds extra layer of security
2. Prevents password phishing
3. Protects against password cracking
Types:
1. SMS-based 2FA
2. Authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Authy)
3. Biometric authentication (fingerprint, facial recognition)
4. Hardware tokens


Benefits:
1. Enhanced security
2. Reduced risk of identity theft
3. Protection against phishing and malware
4. Compliance with security regulations
Securing social media and student accounts.
Social Media Security:
1. Use strong, unique passwords.
2. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA).
3. Set privacy settings to limit who can see your content.
4. Monitor friend requests and connections.
5. Avoid sharing sensitive information.
6. Log out of public computers.
7. Keep software and apps up-to-date.
Student Account Security:
1. Use school-provided email and accounts.
2. Create complex passwords.
3. Enable 2FA (if available).
4. Keep login credentials confidential.
5. Be cautious with links and attachments.
6. Regularly update passwords.
7. Monitor account activity.
Additional Tips:
1. Verify online profiles.
2. Set up account alerts.
3. Use reputable antivirus software.
4. Educate yourself on cyber threats.
5. Report suspicious activity.
    
Module 4: Hacking and Malware

    Objective: To understand hacking threats and how to protect against malware and ransomware.
Content:
Types of hacking attacks targeting students (account hacking, device infiltration).
Students are vulnerable to various types of hacking attacks, including:
Account Hacking:
1. Phishing: Fake emails or messages to steal login credentials.
2. Password Cracking: Using software to guess weak passwords.
3. Keyloggers: Malware that records keystrokes.
4. Session Hijacking: Intercepting login sessions.
Device Infiltration:
1. Malware: Viruses, Trojans, spyware, ransomware.
2. Drive-by Downloads: Malware-infected websites.
3. SQL Injection: Exploiting database vulnerabilities.
4. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS): Injecting malicious code.
Other Attacks:
1. Social Engineering: Manipulating individuals into revealing sensitive info.
2. Wi-Fi Eavesdropping: Intercepting data transmitted over public Wi-Fi.
3. Ransomware: Encrypting files, demanding payment.
4. Denial of Service (DoS): Overwhelming systems with traffic.
Student-Specific Targets:
1. University databases
2. Online learning platforms
3. Student email accounts
4. Personal devices (laptops, smartphones)
5. Cloud storage services
What is malware, spyware, and ransomware?

Malware
Malware, short for "malicious software," refers to any software designed to harm or exploit computer systems. Malware can:
1. Disrupt operations
2. Steal sensitive information
3. Damage files
4. Hijack computer resources
Types of malware:
1. Viruses
2. Worms
3. Trojans
4. Spyware
5. Ransomware
6. Rootkits
7. Botnets
8. Adware
Spyware
Spyware is malware that secretly monitors and collects user data, including:
1. Browsing habits
2. Keystrokes
3. Passwords
4. Sensitive information
Types of spyware:
1. Adware (displays unwanted ads)
2. Keyloggers (records keystrokes)
3. Screen scrapers (captures screen data)
4. Tracking cookies
Ransomware
Ransomware is malware that:
1. Encrypts files
2. Demands payment for decryption
3. Threatens permanent data loss
Types of ransomware:
1. Crypto-ransomware (encrypts files)
2. Locker-ransomware (locks device or account)
3. DoS-ransomware (threatens denial-of-service attacks)
Common ways malware spreads (downloads, email attachments, USB drives).
Common Ways Malware Spreads:
1. Downloads: Infected software, cracked programs, or fake updates.
2. Email Attachments: Malicious files or links in emails.
3. USB Drives: Infected devices or files transferred via USB.
4. Infected Websites: Drive-by downloads or exploit kits.
5. Social Engineering: Phishing, spear phishing, or whaling attacks.
6. Network Vulnerabilities: Unpatched software or weak passwords.
7. Peer-to-Peer (P2P) File Sharing: Infected files shared through P2P networks.
8. Infected Software Updates: Fake or compromised updates.
9. QR Code Scanning: Malicious QR codes leading to infected websites.
10. Public Wi-Fi: Unsecured networks or malicious hotspots.
How to avoid malware and use antivirus tools.
Avoiding Malware:
1. Download software from trusted sources.
2. Verify email attachments and links.
3. Avoid suspicious pop-ups and ads.
4. Keep software and operating systems updated.
5. Use strong passwords and enable two-factor authentication.
6. Be cautious with public Wi-Fi.
7. Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network).
8. Regularly back up data.
Antivirus Tools:
1. Install reputable antivirus software (e.g., Norton, McAfee, Kaspersky).
2. Keep antivirus software updated.
3. Run regular system scans.
4. Enable real-time protection.
5. Use firewall protection.

    Module 5: Online Grooming and Social Engineering

Objective: To educate students on the dangers of online predators and social engineering tactics.
Content:
What is online grooming?
Online grooming is a sinister and predatory practice where individuals, often adults, manipulate and exploit children or teenagers through online platforms, including social media, messaging apps, and gaming communities. Groomers use tactics such as building trust, sharing explicit content, and emotional manipulation to exploit their victims, often with devastating consequences. This exploitation can lead to severe emotional trauma, sexual abuse, and even physical harm. It is crucial for parents, caregivers, and online platforms to be vigilant and proactive in preventing online grooming by educating children about online safety, monitoring their online activities, and reporting suspicious behavior to authorities. By working together, we can protect vulnerable individuals and create a safer online environment.
How predators use social media and online games to target students.
Social Media Tactics:
1. Creating fake profiles to gain trust
2. Sending friend requests to minors
3. Sharing explicit or harmful content
4. Using hashtags to target specific groups
5. Direct messaging or commenting on posts
Online Gaming Tactics:
1. Creating in-game friendships
2. Sharing personalized info or gifts
3. Engaging in voice or video chats
4. Using gaming forums or chat rooms
5. Exploiting game features (e.g., virtual currencies)
What is social engineering, and how it manipulates trust to gather sensitive information.
Social engineering is a manipulative tactic used to deceive individuals into divulging confidential information, gaining unauthorized access, or performing certain actions that compromise security. It exploits human psychology, leveraging cognitive biases, trust, and emotional responses to achieve malicious goals. Social engineers use various techniques, such as phishing, pretexting, and baiting, to create a false sense of urgency, authority, or familiarity, making victims more likely to comply with their requests. This can lead to devastating consequences, including financial loss, identity theft, and compromised sensitive data. By understanding social engineering tactics and cultivating a culture of awareness and skepticism, individuals and organizations can better protect themselves against these insidious threats and safeguard their security and privacy.
Manipulation Techniques:
1. Gaslighting: Making victims question reality.
2. Anchoring: Influencing decisions with initial information.
3. Scarcity: Creating false urgency.
4. Social proof: Using fake testimonials or endorsements.
5. Foot-in-the-door: Requesting small favors before larger ones.
Warning signs and how to protect against manipulation.
Warning Signs:
1. Unsolicited requests for personal info or money
2. Urgent or threatening messages
3. Unusual or suspicious links/attachments
4. Overly friendly or flattering approaches
5. Pressure to make quick decisions
6. Inconsistencies in stories or credentials
7. Emotional appeals or guilt trips
8. Unwillingness to provide clear information
Protection Tips:
Online:
1. Verify sender identities and URLs
2. Be cautious with links and attachments
3. Use strong passwords and 2FA
4. Monitor accounts and report suspicious activity
5. Use antivirus software and firewalls
In-Person:
1. Trust your instincts
2. Set boundaries and assert yourself
3. Verify credentials and references
4. Avoid impulse decisions
5. Seek advice from trusted individuals
Over Phone/Email:
1. Verify caller/sender identities
2. Don't provide sensitive info
3. Be wary of urgent requests
4. Hang up/report suspicious calls
5. Don't respond to suspicious emails
    

        Module 6: Safe Online Practices and Digital Hygiene

        Objective: To instill best practices for maintaining online safety and digital hygiene.
Content:
Importance of digital hygiene (updating software, using secure networks).
Maintaining good digital hygiene is essential in today's interconnected world. Updating software and operating systems regularly, using secure networks, and practicing safe online habits are crucial steps in protecting against cyber threats. Outdated software and weak network security can leave devices vulnerable to malware, data breaches, and hacking. By keeping software up-to-date, using strong passwords, and enabling two-factor authentication, individuals can significantly reduce the risk of cyber attacks. Additionally, using virtual private networks (VPNs) and avoiding public Wi-Fi for sensitive activities can further safeguard online privacy. Regularly backing up data and monitoring account activity also help prevent data loss and financial exploitation. By prioritizing digital hygiene, individuals can ensure their online security, protect sensitive information, and maintain peace of mind in an increasingly digital landscape.
Protecting personal information online.
Secure Online Practices:
1. Use strong, unique passwords and 2FA.
2. Enable privacy settings on social media.
3. Use encryption for sensitive data.
4. Avoid using public Wi-Fi for sensitive activities.
5. Verify website authenticity (HTTPS).
6. Monitor account activity regularly.
7. Use reputable antivirus software.
Data Protection:
1. Limit personal info sharing online.
2. Use secure online storage services.
3. Back up data regularly.
4. Use password managers.
5. Shred sensitive documents.
Identity Protection:
1. Use two-factor authentication.
2. Monitor credit reports.
3. Avoid phishing scams.
4. Use identity theft protection services.
5. Keep personal info private.
Social Media Precautions:
1. Set profiles to private.
2. Avoid sharing sensitive info.
3. Verify friend requests.
4. Use social media monitoring tools.
5. Log out of unused accounts.
Recognizing safe websites and secure connections.
Secure Connection Indicators:
1. HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure) in the URL
2. Padlock icon in the address bar
3. "Secure" or "Trusted" labels
4. Green address bar (Extended Validation Certificate)
Safe Website Characteristics:
1. Clear and concise domain name
2. Professional design and layout
3. Valid contact information
4. Visible privacy policy and terms
5. Trust seals (e.g., VeriSign, TRUSTe)
Awareness of privacy settings on social media and educational platforms.
Social Media Privacy Settings:
1. Facebook: Review settings under "Privacy Center"
2. Instagram: Go to "Settings" > "Account" > "Privacy"
3. Twitter: Visit "Settings" > "Account" > "Privacy"
4. LinkedIn: Check "Settings" > "Account" > "Privacy"
Educational Platform Privacy Settings:
1. Google Classroom: Review "Settings" > "Account" > "Privacy"
2. Canvas: Check "Account" > "Settings" > "Privacy"
3. Blackboard: Visit "Settings" > "Account" > "Privacy"
4. Moodle: Review "Preferences" > "Privacy"
Key Privacy Settings:
1. Profile visibility
2. Post sharing options
3. Friend/connection requests
4. Data sharing with third-party apps
5. Location sharing
6. Contact information visibility
Best Practices:
1. Set profiles to private
2. Limit friend/connection requests
3. Disable location sharing
4. Review data sharing with third-party apps
5. Regularly update privacy settings
6. Monitor account activity
